# Understanding Chargebacks

A chargeback is a transaction reversal meant to serve as a form of consumer protection against fraudulent activity committed by both merchants and individuals.

## Chargeback Reasons
Common reason codes include:
- **Fraudulent Transaction**: The cardholder claims they did not authorize or participate in the transaction.
- **Item Not Received**: The cardholder claims they did not receive the goods or services purchased.
- **Defective Merchandise**: The item received was damaged, defective, or not as described.
- **Processing Error**: A technical glitch caused a duplicate charge or incorrect amount.

## The Chargeback Lifecycle
1. **Initiation**: The cardholder contacts their issuing bank to dispute a charge.
2. **Review**: The issuing bank reviews the dispute and, if valid, initiates the chargeback process through the card network.
3. **Notification**: The acquiring bank receives the chargeback and passes it along to the merchant. The merchant's account is usually debited immediately for the disputed amount plus a chargeback fee.
4. **Representment**: The merchant has the opportunity to fight the chargeback by submitting compiling evidence (receipts, delivery confirmation, customer communication) proving the transaction was valid.
5. **Decision (Pre-Arbitration)**: The issuing bank reviews the merchant's evidence. If they side with the merchant, the funds are returned. If they side with the cardholder, the chargeback stands.
6. **Arbitration**: If the merchant or acquirer disputes the second decision, it can go to the card network (Visa/Mastercard) for a final binding decision.

## Chargeback Fees
Most acquiring banks charge merchants a non-refundable "chargeback fee" (typically ₹500 - ₹2000) simply for the administrative cost of processing the dispute, regardless of whether the merchant wins or loses the representment.
